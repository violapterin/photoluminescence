@@at first glance, the declension of German adjective $seems hard to remember.@@ _
@@there are 3 kinds of declension:@@
   @@strong declension (without any article),@@
   @@mixed declension (with@@ ##ein## @@,@@ ##kein## @@,@@ ##mein## @@,@@ ##sein## @@and so on),@@
   @@and weak declension (with@@ ##der## @@,@@ ##welcher## @@,@@ ##solcher## @@,@@ ##dieser## @@,@@ ##aller## @@and so on).@@ _
@@they #change according to cases nominative [Nom], accusative [Acc], dative [Dat], and genitive [Gen], and genders masculine [Mas], feminine [Fem], neuter [Neut], and plural [Pl],@@
@@so there are 12 combinations.@@ _
@@however, not all of them $has to be memorized.@@ '

@@in strong declension, adjectives always #agree with the corresponding suffix, because they #have to provide the information {which definite articles usually #do}.@@ '

@@in mixed declension, adjectives #agree with the corresponding suffix, only for these 6 items: Nom Mas, Fem, and Neut, and Acc Mas, Fem, and Neut, because some information is already &given by the indefinite articles;@@
@@otherwise, they #end with@@ ##en## @@.@@ _
@@the article $agrees with the corresponding suffix, except for Nom Mas, Nom Neut, and Acc Neut, {where no suffix is &added};@@ _
@@this rule should be known to a beginning learner.@@ '

@@in weak declension, adjectives #end with@@ ##e## @@,@@ @@for these 5 items: Nom Mas, Fem, and Neut, and Acc Fem and Neut, giving even less information;@@
@@otherwise, they #end with@@ ##en## @@.@@ _
@@we #summarize this in the table below.@@ '
